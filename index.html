A Legal Study on the Independence of the Bank of Lebanon

 

and the Necessity of Maintaining the Homogeneity of Banking Legislation

 

This study was prepared with the aim of clarifying the principle of independence inherent to the Bank of Lebanon in accordance with the systems and provisions established in the basic law known as the Code of Money and Credit, promulgated by Decree No. 13513 dated 1/08/1963.

 

We will demonstrate below that Lebanese legislation has enshrined and maintained, for 62 years, i.e., since the enactment of the Code of Money and Credit (in 1963), the independence of the Central Bank, and that it has also maintained the coherence (Cohérence) of banking legislation. However, the draft law related to "reforming the situation of banks in Lebanon and their restructuring," known as the Bank Resolution Law, which was approved by the Council of Ministers on April 12, 2025, directly affects the independence of the Bank of Lebanon and the homogeneity of banking legislation.

 

From this standpoint, this study will also address the extent to which the proposed draft laws and amendments conform to the concept of independence specific to the Central Bank and warn against adopting these drafts due to the potential "legal" risks to the structural integrity (Intégrité structurelle) of the Central Bank, whether through the legislative fragmentation of the regulatory hierarchy of the Central Bank on which it was established, or in terms of their violation (the proposed draft laws) of constitutional, legislative, and administrative principles.

 

The study will be divided into two sections:

 

*   The first section addresses the principle of the independence of the Bank of Lebanon and the correlation of this independence with ensuring its unity, organization, apparatuses, and their interconnectedness in carrying out its functions as an administrative institution independent of any oversight or tutelage from the executive and governmental authorities. It also addresses the principle of the homogeneity of banking legislation.

 

*   The second section will identify the points of legislative fragmentation adopted in the draft law related to reforming the situation of banks in Lebanon and their restructuring, as approved by the Council of Ministers on 2025/04/12, and this project's violation of the principle of independence vested in the Bank of Lebanon.

Section One: The Independence of the Bank of Lebanon

 

1.  The Bank of Lebanon, the central bank of the Lebanese Republic, was established in 1963 by virtue of the law implemented by Decree No. 13513 issued on August 1, 1963, entitled "Code of Money and Credit." This law came as a comprehensive and self-sufficient Code that establishes the Central Bank and regulates the commercial banking sector. Its final article (Article 230) repealed all legal and regulatory provisions contrary to its provisions.

 

2.  The independence of the Bank of Lebanon constitutes a well-established principle explicitly referred to in the explanatory memorandum of the Code of Money and Credit, and affirmed by the various standards contained in the provisions of this law.

 

3.  Indeed, the explanatory memorandum of the draft Code of Money and Credit emphasized the independence of the Bank, stating the following (pages 10 and 11):

 

"When establishing the law and system of the Central Bank, the government's primary concern was the independence and effectiveness of this institution, so that the new institution could carry out its mission impartially and in the direction of the country's permanent interests.

 

...

 

The independence of the Central Bank from political authorities is of the same importance. There is no doubt that granting the privilege of issuance must be surrounded by guarantees, and this is what the provisions of the draft law provide, which regulate the operations of the Central Bank and also regulate state oversight over it. However, neither the granting of the privilege nor the granting of capital by the government should make the Central Bank a subordinate interest to it. Therefore, the project defines the state's relations with the Central Bank. And the government, inspired, on the one hand, by our country's free system, and on the other hand, by the evolution, in the practical field (and despite the texts sometimes) of the concept of relations between public authorities and central banks, even nationalized ones, decided to leave to the future Central Bank more initiative and independence, placing its relations with it on the level of close cooperation that finds its first expression in the composition of the Central Bank Council itself."

4. The Central Bank is an independent public administrative and financial institution (Article 13 of the Code of Money and Credit). In this sense, it enjoys complete independence from the executive and governmental authorities. By granting it legal personality, independent from the personality of the State, the legislator has given the first indication of its intention to grant the Central Bank independence.

 

Reference:

 

Ghaleb Mahmassani, L'organisation bancaire au Liban, Librairie du Liban, 1968, pages 123 et suivantes.

 

The independence granted by the legislator to the Bank of Lebanon is "quasi-total from the government, whether in determining policies or in their implementation." This independence is particularly represented in the delegation granted by the legislator to the Bank of Lebanon, in Article 70 of the Code of Money and Credit, to do what is necessary to maintain the stability (soundness) of the Lebanese currency, as it is known that the basic criterion for the independence of central banks lies in the freedom of movement in matters related to the national currency. The obligation to agree with the Minister of Finance whenever the Bank of Lebanon wants to intervene in the foreign exchange market, whether buying or selling, is only additional proof that the principle is the complete independence of the Bank in its technical and banking activity, and the exclusive and explicit exception is the obligation to agree with the Minister of Finance in this specific case exclusively (in Article 75 of the Code of Money and Credit).

 

Reference:

 

Ghassan El-Ayach, The Central Bank and the State, Union of Arab Banks, 1998, pages 186, 187, and 189.

 

5. The independence of the Central Bank is not merely a transient concept or a declarative phrase, but rather a fundamental pillar stemming from its being a public institution enjoying administrative and financial independence. Therefore, the organization of the Central Bank's bodies, how they are formed and appointed, and the powers granted to it must be studied in detail to determine the extent of its independence.

 

Reference:

 

Samy Chamas, L'état et les systèmes bancaires contemporains, Sirey, 1965, page 132.

6. This independence is based on three main pillars recognized by jurisprudence and confirmed by constitutional decisions, namely:

 

a- Decisional Authority: The independent public institution must enjoy broad and independent powers in regulating the sector for which it was established, and it must carry out its tasks independently of any political or professional authority. This is indeed what the Bank of Lebanon enjoys, as from a regulatory perspective, it is the public legal entity entrusted with organizing, managing, and supervising the financial and banking sector in the country, and ensuring its stability and soundness (Article 70 of the Code of Money and Credit). For this purpose, it exercises the powers and prerogatives of public authority (Prérogatives de puissance publique), i.e., taking unilateral administrative decisions binding on the banking sector (Articles 26, 70, 76, etc. of the Code).

 

Reference:

 

René Chapus, Droit administratif général, Tome 1, 13ème édition, No. 296.

 

The category of independent administrative authorities can only have specificity (and the term authority can only be justified) if it only includes bodies holding, for the very accomplishment of their mission, decision-making power.

 

b- Organizational and Functional Independence: The independent public institution must enjoy broad and independent powers in regulating the sector for which it was established, and it must carry out its tasks independently of any political or professional authority. This is indeed what the Bank of Lebanon enjoys, as from a regulatory perspective, it is the public legal entity solely responsible for organizing, managing, and supervising the financial and banking sector in the country, and ensuring its stability and soundness (Article 70 of the Code of Money and Credit). The Central Bank also enjoys financial independence and is not subject to the rules to which public institutions are subject (Article 13), and special law applies to its operations and employees (Article 33). The relationship of the Bank of Lebanon with the executive authority shows that there is no subordinate relationship: the role of the Minister of Finance; and the presence of the Director General of the Ministry of Finance and the Ministry of Economy in the Central Council (without these two members being able to act in the Council as delegates).

This indicates that the state's allocation of the capital value to the Central Bank (Article 15) does not detract from the independence of the Central Bank, but rather strengthens its position and supports its public-service role. This also explains the provisions of Article 113 of the law, which regulate the distribution of profits and coverage of losses of the Bank of Lebanon. 7.  Furthermore, it is useful to point out an important matter related to the debt securities issued by the Lebanese government. Eurobonds. The independence of the Central Bank from the government is of great importance in cases where the government defaults on paying its dues to its foreign creditors. For example, the immunity enjoyed by foreign central banks in the United States of America, pursuant to the provisions of the Foreign Sovereign Immunities Act of 1976, is granted only for assets held by foreign central banks for their own account (Hold for its own account). This means that only the funds used by foreign central banks to conduct their business are covered by immunity, and anything else is not covered by immunity. If the foreign state waives its immunity (which the Lebanese state did in the Eurobonds contracts, waiving immunity from jurisdiction and immunity from execution), the assets of the Central Bank remain protected by its own immunity. However, if the foreign government acts as a principal to the Central Bank and does not maintain an "At arm's length" relationship, then the Alter Ego theory applies, and the Central Bank loses its immunity over its assets, as it is considered not independent of the government (which waived its immunity). Therefore, it is in Lebanon's supreme interest to preserve the independence of the Bank of Lebanon so that it does not lose its own immunity.

9. All of this indicates that the Lebanese legislator, for decades (since 1963 and at least until 2015, the date of issuance of the law on combating money laundering and terrorist financing), has maintained a logic based on Article 70 of the Code of Money and Credit, which entrusted the Bank of Lebanon with a general mission of "preserving the currency to ensure a basis for sustainable economic and social growth." This mission specifically includes preserving the integrity of the Lebanese currency, economic stability, the soundness of the banking system, and also developing the money market (including payment methods and systems, money transfer operations, clearing and settlement - amendment of Article 70 by Law No. 133 dated 10/26/1999). Based on this logic, the Bank of Lebanon must be granted full capabilities to complete its tasks stipulated in Article 70. In addition to the independence it enjoys, the dispersion of powers between it and other entities must be avoided, which limits the effectiveness of its actions, undermines its mission, and dilutes responsibilities. The independence of the Central Bank is not an absolute concept, but rather it is linked to a specific objective.

10. Therefore, alongside the principle of the independence of the Bank of Lebanon, there is another principle that has been well-established for more than half a century by successive legislators and governments, which we call the "principle of coherence of banking legislation" (Principe de cohérence de la législation bancaire). Every piece of legislation issued after the Code of Money and Credit in the field of banking and finance has maintained a close link with the Code of Money and Credit and with the Bank of Lebanon. This can only be justified by one goal: not to fragment legal provisions and competent authorities, dilute responsibilities, and undermine the national mission of the Bank of Lebanon stipulated in Article 70 of the Code of Money and Credit. If the legislator did not consolidate (Consolider) the laws issued in 1967 and 1970 and others related thereto within the Code of Money and Credit, this does not diminish the connection of these texts to the Code of Money and Credit, and the principle of coherence in banking legislation remains established and constant.

 

11. As a support for the principle of legislative coherence, the Code of Money and Credit was formulated in the form of a "legal code" or a set of provisions related to banking regulation (Code). This is evidenced by the fact that the Code of Money and Credit came as a regulatory framework for the monetary and banking sector in its public (Bank of Lebanon) and private (commercial banks, financial institutions, etc.) branches. It is considered the "supreme organic law" (Loi organique suprême) under which all subsequent laws and regulations must be organized. The Code of Money and Credit was divided into 6 chapters: (1) Money, (2) The Central Bank, (3) Banking Regulation, (4) Penalties, (5) Transitional Provisions, and (6) Miscellaneous and Final Provisions. This code includes a set of interrelated laws and regulations as a cohesive and integrated whole (Corpus). Also, any chapter of this code is organically linked to the other chapters, and the Central Bank is considered the sponsor and cornerstone of this code, and all other chapters revolve in its orbit.

12. The independence of the Central Bank and the Governor is at the core and essence of the 1963 set of laws and the Code of Money and Credit (Code de la Monnaie et du Credit), and therefore:

 

a- Any amendment to the set of regulations and laws must aim for and result in further independence, not its weakening, diminution, fragmentation, or splintering.

 

b- The Governor of the Bank of Lebanon has autonomous administrative and executive powers, specific to him, which rely on independence and hierarchy in decision-making.

 

c- Transferring powers from the Central Bank and the Governorship to any other authority, inside or outside the structure adopted in the 1963 law, exposes the entire banking sector to chaos and instability.

 

d- The Governor possesses supervisory and investigative bodies and arms (committees within the Central Bank) that enable him to perform his work with complete independence and thus prevent interference from political authorities in his work.

 

e- Any reconsideration of the powers and tasks of the departments organically affiliated with the Governor (committees and bodies) at the expense of the Governor and the Central Bank is considered illegal and a blow to the hierarchy of norms (Hiérarchie des normes) pertaining to the Central Bank.

 

f- Any legal amendments to the banking code and the regulations subordinate to the Code of Money and Credit must be consistent with the hierarchy adopted in the interrelated texts. Consequently, any amendments to the tasks of the Governor, the Central Council, or the stipulated committees or bodies cannot ignore the general principles, especially the principle of hierarchy and the principle of independence, which is considered the essence of independent banking regulation.

 

g- This independence was deliberately granted to the Central Council and the Governor specifically to avoid any political or external interference, with the aim of ensuring the stability of the currency and the soundness of the banking system.

d- The Governor of the Central Bank is considered one of the key figures in the state. He is appointed for a specific term during which he cannot be dismissed except for specific reasons (resignation, illness, gross misconduct). Consequently, after their appointment, the Governor of the Bank of Lebanon and his deputies cannot be dismissed except in strictly limited cases (Article 19); and the Central Council is the one that establishes their special regulations (Article 33, paragraph 9), and it also ensures broad independence for them and for the internal structure of the Central Bank.

 

13. From this perspective, any amendment to the set of banking laws must necessarily take into account the hierarchy adopted in the founding texts, in addition to preserving and emphasizing the independence of the Central Bank and its decisions emanating from the Governor. Consequently, any connection with the political and executive authorities of the state, such as granting decision-making powers to the Council of Ministers or the Minister of Finance, who is part of the political authority, must be avoided. In principle, the Minister has no authority in the narrow sense over independent public administrative authorities (Autorités administratives indépendantes), foremost among them the Central Bank. At the very least, there is no exercise of any hierarchical supervisory authority or tutelage by government agencies.

 

14. However, the principle of independence of the Central Bank does not mean that it is an isolated island within the state or government work. The government or the competent minister has a presence (Présence) in the work of the Central Bank through the appointment of a government commissioner by the Ministry of Finance at the Central Bank, as shown below:

 

a- Article 41 of the Code of Money and Credit stipulates that a "Government Commissariat at the Central Bank" shall be established in the Ministry of Finance. This department is managed by an employee with the rank of Director General (first category) who holds the title of "Government Commissioner at the Central Bank."

 

b- As stated in Article 43 of the same law, the decisions of the Council are immediately communicated to the Government Commissioner. He has, within the two days following notification, the right to request the Governor to suspend any decision he deems contrary to the law and regulations and to consult the Minister of Finance in this regard. If the matter is not decided within five days from the date of

Having clarified the principle of the independence of the Central Bank from both organizational and functional aspects (Indépendance statutaire et fonctionnelle), we reiterate and emphasize that this independence can only be understood and applied usefully and effectively if it encompasses and extends to all bodies, departments, authorities, and committees that are also organically and functionally linked to the Central Council and the Governor. This is what can be called complete and integrated unity, and any destabilization in the organizational structure or the basic tasks of the affiliated bodies exposes the integrity of the Central Bank to danger, which is considered a violation of the principle of integrity (Violation du principe d'intégrité).

18. The French Constitutional Council, which is the highest judicial authority that preserves fundamental constitutional principles and rights, including the independence of the Central Bank enshrined in laws, considers this independence a general principle of monetary and banking stability in the country. Consequently, any law or amendment that affects this principle exposes the law to challenge and annulment before the Constitutional Council. Also, any decree or ministerial decision contrary to the principle of independence and the principle of non-interference of the executive authority in the work of the Central Bank exposes this decree or decision to challenge and annulment before the State Council, due to its conflict with the laws and legal principles stipulated in the set of laws regulating the monetary and banking sector, foremost among which is the principle of independence and non-exposure of the government to the proper functioning of the sector.

 

19. The Code of Money and Credit and the legal framework emanating from it are considered, as we mentioned earlier, constitutive and substantive (Organique) laws that take precedence over other ordinary laws, as they establish, regulate, and define the system of the monetary and banking sector in Lebanon, and also regulate the Central Bank, its structure, and its powers. From this perspective, the basic statute (Statut) of the Central Bank, as stipulated by the Code of Money and Credit in its second chapter, is equivalent to an organic law (Loi organique) as it lays down the constitutive provisions for the Central Bank as a public administrative institution that oversees the monetary and banking sector in the country.

 

20. The Central Bank, as a legal entity of public law, enjoys financial independence and is therefore not subject to the rules of administration, conduct of business, and supervision to which public sector institutions are subject (Article 13 of the Code of Money and Credit), especially the provisions and legislative decrees Nos. 114, 115, 117, 118 dated June 12, 1959. Thus, the legislator has exempted the Central Bank from the supervision to which departments are subject.

21. The establishment and organization of the Central Bank in 1963 is a primary organization and not the result of previous laws. Before independence, Lebanon was subject to Ottoman law, and during the French Mandate, the Bank of Syria and Lebanon was the bank, and the adopted currency was the Syrian-Lebanese Lira. After independence in 1943, the Bank of Syria and Lebanon became (in 1948) the accredited bank for issuing the Lebanese Lira and was a private institution under French patronage. From this perspective, the establishment of the Bank of Lebanon in 1963 under the second chapter of the Code of Money and Credit is equivalent to an organic law (Loi organique) that defines its structure and independence, as well as all other subordinate, sponsoring, and protecting laws, which collectively must be consistent and harmonious with the provisions and principles set forth in the law, the most important of which is the principle of general independence, especially from the executive authority (the government). Therefore, any amendment cannot have legal legitimacy unless it comes within this structure and provisions.

 

22. The independence of the Bank of Lebanon is also reflected in the legitimate priority of the Central Bank in maintaining the integrity of the currency, economic stability, and the soundness of the banking system (Article 70 of the Code of Money and Credit). Accordingly, the Central Bank is a primary, active, and necessary partner in any modernization process by the governmental and legislative authorities that affects the Code. From this perspective, Articles 71 and 72 of the Code of Money and Credit, which require the Central Bank to cooperate with the government and provide it with advice related to monetary and economic policy, must be read and interpreted. Article 72 also clarified that the government must consult the Bank on matters related to currency and invite the Governor of the Bank to participate in its deliberations on these issues. This confirms the binding nature of consulting the Central Bank for any modernization or development of the banking system, as the Central Bank is considered primarily and ultimately responsible for defining and maintaining the integrity of monetary and financial policy in the country (Articles 33 and 70 of the Code of Money and Credit).

23. Banking regulations and central banks worldwide are among the most independent state institutions, touching upon the principle of sovereignty enshrined in some countries (see French Law No. 357 dated 05/12/1998, which amended the French Central Bank system after France joined the European System of Central Banks and transferred sovereign powers in the monetary and banking sector to the Governing Council of the European Central Bank (ECB)). This Article (38), with all its flaws (how can the new law replace previous laws, and at the same time use bodies established and regulated in previous laws?), was deleted from the final draft dated April 12, 2025, which was approved by the Council of Ministers.

 

60. Furthermore, the "Reform Draft Law" included a provision in its Article 23 stating that the provisions of Chapter 9 of this law apply exclusively to the liquidation process and replace any laws and other regulations that conflict with its content. Consequently, the "Reform Draft Law" has further fragmented the laws regulating the Bank of Lebanon and guaranteeing its independence. It is clear from this article that it specified that the ninth chapter of the "Reform Draft Law" related to the liquidation process replaces any laws and other regulations that conflict with its content, but it did not, in return, specify any legal precedence for the rest of its articles and chapters, despite their conflict with the laws regulating the Bank of Lebanon and other commercial laws that are directly related to the texts of this project, especially, for example, Article 16 of its fifth chapter, which defines and expands the powers of the Higher Banking Authority, which has become solely concerned with the process of reforming the situation of banks in isolation from the Bank of Lebanon, which originally did not have those powers. Did this Authority replace the Bank of Lebanon with new powers? Or did it remain under its supervision and regulations from a purely illusory perspective? We hope that this study has shed light on the extent and importance of the independence of the Bank of Lebanon to fortify its role and basic tasks in preserving the integrity of the Lebanese currency, economic stability, the soundness of the banking system, and developing the monetary and financial market (Article 70 of the Code of Money and Credit). It has also shed light on the extent and importance of the principle of homogeneity of banking legislation, which the legislator has maintained, despite all difficulties, for 62 years (since the issuance of the Code of Money and Credit in 1963).

 

We also hope that this study has shown how the draft law related to "Reforming the Situation of Banks in Lebanon and Their Restructuring" in its current form, which was approved by the Council of Ministers on April 12, 2025, directly affects the two basic principles upon which the Bank of Lebanon and the entire banking sector were built: the independence of the Bank of Lebanon and the homogeneity of banking legislation.
